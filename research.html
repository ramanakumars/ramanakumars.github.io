Moist convection on Jupiter

I am working on using the EPIC GCM (Dowling et al., 2006) to study cloud formation near the 24-degree N jet on Jupiter. Several decades of observation of this region has shown convective storms which disrupt the structure of the jet and also change the chromophoric structure of the nearby zone and belt (see e.g., Sánchez-Lavega et al., 2017). These plumes are a result of moist convection due to release of latent heat from water vapor deep in the atmosphere. I am working on adding a moist convective module to EPIC to study the formation of these plumes and understand their dynamics and how they affect the atmosphere.

The effect of cloud condensation on Ice Giants

The Great Dark Spot was a large scale optically dark feature observed by Voyager 2 in 1989 during its flyby of Neptune. Several bright companion clouds were also observed in the vicinity of the spot and the spot itself drifted equatorward while having a periodic oscillation in shape and size. I helped run simulations using the EPIC GCM with its cloud microphysics module to understand the effect of latent heat release and cloud formation on the dynamical behaviour of the spots. We found that the actively forming clouds changed both the shape oscillation and the drift rate of the vortex, much more so than just adding methane vapor to the atmosphere.

I have also run simulations and helped with analysis of similar features on Uranus. We found that the presence of the vortex leads to orographic upwelling resulting in cloud formation near the vortices.

Fragmentation modelling of meteor impacts

On August 19th, 2019, Ethan Chappel observed an impact flash on Jupiter's SEB with a sufficiently high cadence to produce a lightcurve. We analysed the lightcurve to obtain the energy deposition, but inferring the geometry of the impactor was not possible from a single video source. Therefore, we developed a fragmentation model, based on Wheeler et al. (2018) and tested different impact geometries and material composition to determine the most probable case. The fragmentation model showed that the impactor at least as dense as a stony asteroid (~2 g/cc) but could be denser if the velocity was lower (~60 km/s).

We are also working on using this model for Earth impactors. On Earth, the trajectory can be determined by triangulating the postion of the bolide from multiple cameras, providing an energy deposition profile as a function of height. Using this information, it is much easier to constrain the material composition of the impactor and its initial mass.

Meteor trajectories from allsky cameras

The Spalding Allsky Camera Network (SACN) is a network for cameras around the world for the purpose of detecting and studying atmospheric impactors. On June 2, 2016, a large fireball was observed over Arizona, and fragments were recovered on the ground from the impact. We used three SACN cameras and one dashcam video to determine the trajectory and pre-atmospheric orbit of the incoming object. Furthermore, using a Multispectral Radiometer (MSR) stationed in Albuquerque, NM, we obtained a high-precision lightcurve of the event and used a fragmentation model to determine the material composition and mass of the impacting object.

Currently, I am helping improve this analysis by adding data provided by the Geostationary Lightning Mapper (GLM) aboard the GOES-16 and 17 satellites. These detectors are able to see bolides brighter than magnitude -14, and so serve as another data point in triangulating the positions of these bolides. We are currently testing the feasibility of the trajectory as determined by triangulating the event from the two satellites, compared to using allsky cameras.
